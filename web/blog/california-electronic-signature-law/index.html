California Electronic Signature Law: UETA Plus Extra Rules

compliance · June 8, 2026 · 4 min read · 871 words
Share X LinkedIn

California says yes to electronic signatures, and then it layers on privacy rules that most senders never see coming.

California electronic signature law looks simple at first glance, since the state made electronic signatures legal back in 1999. There is a second layer most people miss, though, because California stacked its own privacy rules on top, and those rules shape how you collect and store signer data. The result is that a contract signed by a Californian follows two distinct sets of rules at once. In this post you will learn the base signing rules, the privacy layer that quietly trips senders up, and the short list of documents California still keeps on paper. By the end you will know exactly what changes the moment a California resident signs your deal.

California electronic signature law starts with UETA

Start with the foundation, because California adopted the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act, or UETA, in 1999, and you can find it codified in California Civil Code section 1633.1. What does it actually do? It gives electronic signatures the same legal force as pen-and-paper signatures for routine contracts, which means that under California UETA an electronic signature is every bit as binding as a wet one. Four tests decide whether a signature holds up, and a contract that satisfies all four stands on solid ground. First comes intent to sign, then consent to do business electronically, then a clear association between the signer and the document, and finally retention of a usable record. Let me make that concrete: your client clicks to sign a coaching agreement, agrees to go electronic, the signature is tied to their email, and you keep the signed PDF, and that single flow satisfies all four tests at once. There is a bonus worth knowing too. California state agencies must accept electronic signatures for almost every official filing, with only narrow exceptions such as documents that need a notary or a witness. As a result, California eSign rules lean firmly in your favor for the bulk of everyday business.

The privacy layer that catches senders off guard

Here is where California diverges from most other states, because it added a privacy layer that they simply do not have. Two laws drive that layer: the CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) and its update, the CPRA, and they take effect when a California consumer signs a contract that collects or uses their personal information. What counts as personal information? A name, an email, an address, or a phone number, which is precisely the data a signing flow captures every single time. Those laws hand the signer real rights, and that is the part senders underestimate. Where California still says no to electronic signing

California does not permit electronic signatures on everything, and a short list of documents still requires the traditional process. Wills, codicils (updates to a will), and trusts still demand formal witnessing, so you cannot simply click to sign them. The stakes are high enough that the law deliberately preserves the in-person step. Real estate documents that require notarization still need a notary as well, though the good news is that California now allows remote online notarization for many of these, which lets the notary join by video so you may not have to drive anywhere. Watch for special cases too. Some consumer transactions fall under specific California statutes, such as certain finance disclosures, and those carry extra requirements beyond UETA. A car loan or a payday-style agreement, for instance, can have its own rules layered on top of the general framework. The takeaway is straightforward. For your normal business contracts, electronic signing works across California without complication, while wills, certain notarized real estate, and a short list of consumer finance documents call for checking the specific rule first. Why do these carve-outs exist at all? Because they protect people in high-stakes moments, since a will decides who inherits and a home sale moves a large amount of money, so the law adds a human step there on purpose. For your service agreements, NDAs, and statements of work, none of that applies, which means you can sign and send the same day. Keep this distinction in mind, and California electronic signature law stops feeling like a maze, because most of what you send is covered and only a small, predictable set needs extra care.
