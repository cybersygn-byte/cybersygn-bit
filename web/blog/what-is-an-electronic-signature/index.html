What is an electronic signature, exactly?

May 26, 2026

Most people use 'electronic signature' loosely, but the legal definition is specific and worth knowing.

An electronic signature is any electronic sound, symbol, or process attached to a document with the intent to sign it. That intent is the part that matters legally — not the format of the signature itself. We'll break down what counts, what doesn't, and how the technology behind modern e-signature platforms actually works.

The legal definition

Under the ESIGN Act (United States, 2000) and UETA (adopted in 49 states), an electronic signature is defined as 'an electronic sound, symbol, or process attached to or logically associated with a contract or other record and executed or adopted by a person with the intent to sign the record.' The key phrase is 'intent to sign.' Typing your name into a checkbox counts. Drawing your signature on a tablet counts. Clicking 'I agree' on a contract counts. The format is flexible; the intent is required.

What separates electronic signatures from digital signatures

These two terms get used interchangeably, but they mean different things. An electronic signature is the legal concept above. A digital signature is a specific cryptographic technique that uses public-key cryptography to verify the signer's identity and detect tampering. Every digital signature is an electronic signature, but not every electronic signature is digital. Modern e-signature platforms often use digital signatures under the hood while presenting a friendlier interface to the signer.

Why this matters for your contracts

If you send contracts for signature, your method needs to capture clear intent, attribute the signature to the right person, preserve the document's integrity, and produce records you can show if challenged. A typed-name-on-a-Word-doc fails most of these. A dedicated e-signature platform handles them automatically — magic links verify the recipient, every event is logged with timestamp and IP, and a hash fingerprint of the document detects tampering.
