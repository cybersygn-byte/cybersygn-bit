Electronic signature vs digital signature: the difference, in plain English

June 2, 2026

If you've ever wondered whether your signing platform produces 'real' digital signatures, you're asking the right question.

An electronic signature is any electronic mark of intent. A digital signature is a specific cryptographic mechanism. Most signed documents in business contexts are electronic signatures; a smaller subset are also digital signatures. The difference matters when stakes are high.

Electronic signatures: the legal category

Electronic signature is a legal term. The ESIGN Act defines it as any electronic sound, symbol, or process attached to a record with intent to sign. Typing your name, drawing your signature, clicking an 'I agree' button — all count. This is the umbrella category that includes everything from a typed name in an email to a notarized digital seal.

Digital signatures: the cryptographic mechanism

Digital signatures are a specific technology using public-key cryptography. A signer's private key generates a unique hash of the document; anyone with the signer's public key can verify the signature came from the corresponding private key. This provides three guarantees: identity (only the keyholder could sign), integrity (any document change invalidates the signature), and non-repudiation (the signer can't deny it later). PDF digital signatures embed this verification directly into the document.

Which one you need depends on the document

For most business contracts, sales agreements, and employment forms, a standard electronic signature is sufficient and legally binding. For high-stakes financial transactions, government filings in some jurisdictions, or documents that need to verify long after the signer is reachable, a digital signature backed by a public-key infrastructure adds an extra layer. Most modern e-signature platforms use digital signatures under the hood while presenting a friendly electronic-signature interface.
